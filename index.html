Part II - A/B Test¶

Notice that because of the time stamp associated with each event, you could technically run a hypothesis test continuously as each observation was observed.

However, then the hard question is do you stop as soon as one page is considered significantly better than another or does it need to happen consistently for a certain amount of time? How long do you run to render a decision that neither page is better than another?

These questions are the difficult parts associated with A/B tests in general.

Question 1.¶

For now, consider you need to make the decision just based on all the data provided. If you want to assume that the old page is better unless the new page proves to be definitely better at a Type I error rate of 5%, what should your null and alternative hypotheses be? You can state your hypothesis in terms of words or in terms of $p_{old}$ and $p_{new}$, which are the converted rates for the old and new pages.

We are assuming that the new page is no better, or even worse than the old page at converting people; this is the null ($H_{0}$). Therefore, the alternative ($H_{1}$) is that the new page is better than the old page at converting people, which is the thing I am trying to prove.

To be more precise, we are assuming the conversion rate of customers who received the new page is no better, or even worse than the conversion rate of customers who received the old page.

Thus, the hypothesis is as follows:

$$\Large H_{0}: p_{new} - p_{old} \leq 0$$
$$\Large H_{1}: p_{new} - p_{old} > 0$$

Q2k.¶

In words, explain what you just computed in 2j above. What is this value called in scientific studies? What does this value mean in terms of whether or not there is a difference between the new and old pages?

Answer:

In question 2j we computed the p-value.

Consider:

p_diffs is an array that contains all of the simulated differences in proportions between conversion rates of the new and old page. It is the sampling distribution.

obs_diff is a statistic; a value where we took the proportion of users that converted when grouped by the treatment (new page) group and the proportion of users that converted when grouped by the control (old page) group and subtracted them to get the observed difference in proportions.

p_diffs > obs_diff is essentially taking every individual value from the p_diffs array, and comparing it to the value of obs_diff to see if the array values are larger. It then marks it as true or false. By using .mean(), we are getting the proportion of True values compared to the whole. This is the p-value.

The p-value of 0.90, or 90%, is strong evidence that the statistic came from the null, and that the null hypothesis is true; the new page is no better at converting customers than the old page. It far exceeds the Type 1 error threshold (the alpha - $\large \alpha$) of 5%, so it is not statistically significant. We fail to reject the null.

Q1e.¶

What is the p-value associated with ab_page? Why does it differ from the value you found in Part II?

Hint: What are the null and alternative hypotheses associated with your regression model, and how do they compare to the null and alternative hypotheses in the Part II?

Link to Part II

Answer:

The p-value associated with ab_page is 0.190. In Part II it was 0.9066.

The reason the p-values are different between Part II and Part III has to do with the hypotheses used for each model. The alternative hypothesis dictates which type of test is used.

 

In Part II, the hypothesis is as follows:

$\large H_{0}: p_{new} - p_{old} \leq 0$
$\large H_{1}: p_{new} - p_{old} > 0$

When the hypothesis is set up this way, it is a one-tailed test (specifically a right-tailed test). The test is directional; it is testing whether the conversion rate for the new page was greater than the old page or not. The A/B Test is using the explanatory variables of the treatment (new page) and control (old page) groups to predict the response variable, the conversion rate.

 

In Part III, the hypothesis is as follows:

$\large H_{0}: p_{new} - p_{old} = 0$
$\large H_{1}: p_{new} - p_{old} \neq 0$

When the hypothesis is set up this way, it is a two-tailed test. It is non-directional; it is testing whether the new page has a conversion rate that is the same as the old page or not. If the conversion rates are not equal, then the landing pages have could have a positive or negative impact, which is why this is a two-tailed test. The logistic regression is measuring the probabilities of the conversion rate given the two possible landing pages.

Conclusions¶


For this project, I used three different methods to determine if the landing page had an impact on the number of customers who "convert" meaning they pay for the company's product:

  • Probability Testing
  • A/B Testing
  • Logistic Regression Testing

Probability Results

In the probability tests I found:

  • The probability of someone converting regardless of group was 11.96%
  • The probability of someone converting from the control group (the old page) was 12.04%
  • The probability of someone converting from the treatment group (the new page) was 11.88%

With a conversion rate of ~12% for each group, I conclude that the landing page his little to no impact on conversion rates.

A/B Test Results

For the A/B testing, I created a hypothesis:

$\large H_{0}: p_{new} - p_{old} \leq 0$
$\large H_{1}: p_{new} - p_{old} > 0$

I created a sampling distribution of the difference in proportions using 10,000 samples, then compared it to the observed difference in proportions. I found that ~90% of the distribution was greater than the observed difference, which is the p-value, indicating that the statistic came from the null, and is therefore not significant. The result is that I fail to reject the null, so the landing page has little to no impact on conversion rates. </font>

Logistic Regression Results

For the logistic regression testing, the hypothesis was as follows:

$\large H_{0}: p_{new} - p_{old} = 0$
$\large H_{1}: p_{new} - p_{old} \neq 0$

I ran three different logistic regression models, comparing conversion rates with: </font>

  • landing page
  • country
  • both landing page and country combined

The results of each of these three tests yielded similar results. The p-values for each exceed the $\large \alpha$ of 0.05, and the coefficients only had ~1% impact on the conversion rate each time. This leads to the same conclusion; neither landing page, country, or a combination of the two has any significant impact on conversion rates.

Final Summary

To summarize this project, each test confirmed the same result; that landing page has little to no impact on conversion rates. The experiment ran for 22 days, and yielded 290,584 unique users to run the tests against. With that many datapoints to work with, extending the experiment duration is unnecessary. Based on these results, implementing the new page or keeping the old page would be a purely aesthetic preference.
